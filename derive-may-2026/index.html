Section 1

Why Options Are the Bigger PrizeThe largest derivatives category on earth, hiding in plain sight

For most Crypto-natives, “derivatives” means perpetual futures. That perception reflects how onchain trading evolved, not how global finance actually works. Equity options alone traded $4 trillion in notional volume per day in 2025 — a 42% increase year-on-year — and finished the year with 552 million open contracts, up 18% on 2024. The CBOE reported a sixth consecutive annual record. This is not a niche product for Tradfi.

The reason is structural. Options are the most flexible financial instrument ever invented. A perpetual gives you a leverage slider — up or down, more or less. An option lets you express a precise opinion: that Bitcoin will land between $80,000 and $90,000 in two months; that the volatility of an asset will be lower next quarter than the market implies; that you are willing to be assigned at a specific price in exchange for premium. Each of those is a different trade, and each one prices differently. That is why JP Morgan still runs a structured products desk and why volatility-trading desks are some of the most profitable seats on Wall Street.

It pays to be specific when you're expressing an opinion in the financial markets. You can earn more money if you're correct. That is finance. It's truly expressive and variable.

Nick Forster, CEO and Founder, Derive
The Edge Podcast, May 2026

Crypto’s options market is already enormous — and lopsided

Crypto derivatives reached approximately $8.94 trillion in monthly volume in 2025, accounting for roughly 76% of all Crypto trading. Options specifically are now growing in step with the equity options boom of the 1990s. In a single trading session in March 2026, Deribit alone cleared $14 billion in options volume. CME’s Crypto futures and options volumes hit $3 trillion in notional for 2025, with Q4 activity up 92% year-on-year.

The lopsidedness is the opportunity. Roughly 85% of all Bitcoin and Ether options globally trade on one venue, Deribit. There is no parallel concentration in spot or perpetuals. Coinbase recognised the prize — and paid for it. The remaining question is who builds the onchain equivalent.

Section 2

Why Institutions Reach for Options, Not PerpsDifferent instrument, different audience, different stickiness

Perpetual futures are extraordinary instruments and, fun fact, they were a Crypto not a Tradfi invention as far back as 2016. They are also a leverage slider with a liquidation price. For a retail discretionary trader chasing a daily move, that is precisely what is wanted. For an institution managing a mandate, it is almost the opposite of what is needed.

An institution holding Bitcoin or ETH typically wants three things: to generate yield against the position, to hedge specific tail risks, and to express a precise view that may take three to twelve months to play out. None of these maps cleanly to a perpetual. All of them map naturally to an option.

10–20%annualised yield
A large fund holding Bitcoin can sell covered calls to generate 10 to 20% annualised yield on the position. The same fund can sell cash-secured puts at a strike where it is already happy to accumulate, earning premium whether or not the trade is assigned. These trades scale to a billion dollars in capital. The basis trade — until recently the dominant source of crypto yield — does not.
Why the Payoff Shapes Matter — Perpetual vs Call Option
The single most important difference for an institution managing a mandate: the option’s downside is known, capped and finite. The perp’s is not. Illustrative.

The yield rotation: from basis trades to volatility

Between 2020 and 2025, two strategies dominated crypto yield generation. The first was the basis trade — long spot, short perpetual — which at peak yielded 15 to 30% almost risk-free. The second was pre-TGE points farming, where funds posted TVL in exchange for token allocations that could be hedged out using pre-market perps. Both were good trades. Good trades that did not last.

Ethena scaled the basis trade with $15 billion of capital and compressed the yield to near the risk-free rate. The market crash on 10 October 2025 was, in Nick Forster’s words, “the final nail in the coffin” — collapsing alt valuations and ending the TVL-for-tokens meta in one move. A swathe of Crypto-native funds built around those strategies have shut down or pivoted; Ethena itself announced a broader strategy rotation away from pure basis in early 2026.

Volatility selling is the natural replacement. It is evergreen — every volatile asset generates option premium, every year. It is bespoke — you can tailor risk from a conservative 2% APY to an aggressive 50%, depending on mandate. And critically, it is the only yield source where institutional size genuinely matters: a $50 million options position commands tighter pricing than a $50,000 one, where a $50 million basis position simply compresses its own returns.

Options are like a machine which turns the volatility of an asset into yield. It's evergreen — for any asset with volatility, you can sell options and generate yield now, in one year, and in ten years.

Nick Forster, CEO, Derive
Yieldmageddon, Derive Insights, 2026

Why option market share is so sticky

The most important property of options markets — and the reason Deribit was worth $2.9 billion — is that they exhibit network effects that simply do not exist in the same way in perpetuals. Three reasons matter.

First, duration. A perpetual closes the moment you click. An option expiring in six months ties capital to the venue for six months — for both the buyer and the market maker on the other side. A market maker quoting a year-dated option on Exchange A will quote it tighter than the same trade on Exchange B simply because their existing positions on A reduce the marginal cost of capital. That asymmetry compounds.

Second, breadth. A liquid perp market needs one product per asset. A liquid option market needs an entire board — every strike, every expiry. By Forster’s estimate, roughly 500 to 1,000 instruments need to be liquid before a market can claim true depth. Building that does not happen in a token-incentive programme. It happens slowly, with anchor institutions and reputation.

Third, the audience. Options buyers and sellers are slower-moving, more institutional, and less promotional. Once onboarded, they do not switch venues for a token reward. They switch only when execution materially improves or counterparty risk forces them. Coinbase recognised this when it paid eleven figures for Deribit’s user base — that exact stickiness is what the cheque was buying.

Section 3

What Derive Actually IsAn onchain options exchange built like a real derivatives venue

Derive is the largest onchain options exchange globally. It runs on the Derive Chain — an OP-Stack appchain optimised specifically for the compute-heavy maths required to margin options at institutional scale. The architecture is hybrid by design: an off-chain Central Limit Order Book and integrated RFQ (Request-for-Quote) system enable price discovery, while all margining, settlement, liquidations and clearing logic execute trustlessly on chain. Users retain self-custody at every stage.

The protocol supports options on Bitcoin, Ethereum, Solana and Hyperliquid (HYPE), alongside perpetuals across these and a wider set of altcoins, plus spot and integrated borrow-lend functionality. Cross-margin sits across the lot — meaning a single institutional account can run an options portfolio, a perp hedge and a borrow position against the same collateral, with portfolio-level margining akin to what a prime broker offers in TradFi.

Founding team

Derive was founded in 2021 by Nick Forster, a former equity options trader at Susquehanna International Group (SIG) — one of the most respected derivatives trading firms in the world, where he traded US equity options through the GameStop short squeeze, the COVID volatility regime and several other notable events. His co-founder Dom Romanowski brought blockchain engineering depth from the Synthetix ecosystem. The product team has since been strengthened by Hitesh, formerly head of product at Trade Paradigm (the institutional RFQ platform connected to Deribit) and a TradeWeb veteran — a hire that directly catalysed the recent inflection in institutional flow.

We've always been like, focused on staying alive and sustainability — that's what we had this vision way back at the start and knew it was going to come to fruition at some point, with strong conviction, but the timing was always a little bit uncertain. So the name of the game for us was always: do things that aren't necessarily the flashiest thing, chasing trends, jumping on things, or shipping things out too quickly, but stay, compound, stay alive and survive.

Nick Forster, CEO, Derive
Interview, January 2026

Architecture in plain English

If you want to buy 100 Bitcoin worth of options, you can either lift the order book or ping the RFQ. The RFQ broadcasts your intent to a set of integrated market makers (Falcon X, GSR and others), each of whom returns a quote within seconds. You accept the best one. The trade settles on the Derive Chain, the protocol locks the appropriate collateral, and the position is now visible, auditable and self-custodied. Liquidations, if they happen, are run by the same trustless margining logic — no manual intervention, no opaque calls from a centralised risk desk.

This sounds simple. It is, in fact, the hardest thing in the category — which is why no one else has built it. Margining an options book at portfolio level requires real-time scenario analysis across hundreds of instruments and Greeks. Running that on chain, deterministically, without breaking under volatility, is what took Derive five years and two architectural rebuilds.

Section 7

Momentum and CatalystsWhy the next 6 to 18 months matter most

Several catalysts are in train, any of which could materially accelerate the curve. Some are deterministic; others are second-order effects of the broader market structure.

RWA options — the long-tail expansion

Derive’s product roadmap explicitly targets options on real-world assets. Forster describes this category — tokenised equities, FX, commodities — as “a blue-ocean wrapper” for the assets coming on chain. The thesis is simple: every useful asset that gets tokenised becomes a candidate for option strategies, and Derive’s shared risk engine means a single integration can carry the entire suite of yield, hedging and speculation tooling that exists today for Bitcoin and ETH. The team is being deliberate — “measure twice and cut once” — but the architectural groundwork is in place.

Integrations and the “infrastructure layer” thesis

Derive’s long-term distribution model is not a retail trading UI. It is being the backend for everyone else’s. Dream OS (mobile, live), several stealth-stage structured product builders, and an undisclosed neobank with hundreds of millions in weekly deposits — each takes Derive’s liquidity and repackages it into a user experience their audience actually wants. This is the right strategy for a category where building from scratch is structurally hard, and where the integrations themselves further entrench the liquidity moat.

AI-native finance

Options are perhaps the single best financial product for AI agents to deploy on behalf of users. The reason is that the friction point of options — knowing how to translate an opinion into the right structure — is exactly what large language models are good at. A user can express a view (“I think Bitcoin will be between $80–90k in eight weeks”) and an agent can structure the corresponding trade as a call spread, ratio spread or any combination that maximises expected payoff. Derive’s self-custodial, verifiable architecture is uniquely well suited to letting agents execute autonomously — exactly the kind of capability that begins to matter when even modest market share shifts that way.

Macro tailwinds

The basis trade is dead. Pre-TGE farming is dead. The two yield sources that crowded out options as a viable institutional product through 2024–2025 are no longer competitive. Volatility selling becomes the natural replacement and Derive is the onchain venue best positioned to absorb that flow. Combined with the Deribit acquisition creating venue churn, the macro setup is unusually favourable.

Section 8

What Could Go Wrong

We feel Derive as an investment is higher than average risk / smaller than average size relative to our other Fund holdings. Despite our high conviction and the significant upside we see, our disciplined risk control matrix limits the size of this position. At Dragonfly, we take a long-term view and continuously monitor the fundamentals against the balance of risk and reward over time. The luxury of investing in liquid tokens means we can sell the position if risks materially increase. Against this, as long-term investors, we intend to fully enjoy the compounded growth potential should Derive achieve its promise, meaning even a small allocation can significantly boost Fund returns. The principal risks are as follows.

Volume reverts before institutional flywheel locks in

The Q1 2026 surge is real, but it is recent. If macro volatility compresses and institutional onboarding slows, daily volumes could pull back toward late-2025 levels. Mitigant: the protocol is now lindy, the team is well-capitalised, and the integrator pipeline (vaults, mobile apps, structured products) does not depend on continued volume growth to ship.

Hyperliquid expands into options

Hyperliquid has signalled options ambitions (a future HIP could add binary options or full options). However, building portfolio-margin infrastructure for options without sacrificing perp leverage is a hard trade-off — Binance failed to take meaningful share from Deribit despite enormous resources, and the focus required to compete is significant. We see Hyperliquid as more likely to integrate with Derive (as backend liquidity) than to displace it. Forster has been explicit about this being his preferred outcome. Any integration turns this potential risk on its head.

Smart-contract risk

Despite the five-year clean record, no DeFi protocol is risk-free and early signals are that AI tools are significantly increasing the risk of hacks in 2026. The architecture has been audited multiple times and includes the onchain insurance fund as a primary backstop. We monitor security disclosures continuously.

Regulatory pressure on onchain derivatives

Crypto derivatives sit in a regulatory grey zone in several jurisdictions, including parts of the US. Self-custodial design and the appchain architecture provide some insulation, but a hostile ruling in a major jurisdiction would compress addressable market. We weight this as a tail risk rather than a base case, given the constructive direction of US regulation under the current administration.

Liquidity concentration in BTC/ETH/HYPE

Roughly 96% of options volume on Derive sits in BTC and ETH today. The RWA and altcoin expansion is the right answer, but its execution is necessarily slower than perps — options need a full board, not just a single pair. Mitigant: the team has been explicit that “measure twice and cut once” is the strategy, and the institutional onboarding pipeline does not require long-tail breadth to scale.
