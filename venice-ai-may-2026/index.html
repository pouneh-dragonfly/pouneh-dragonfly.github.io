Executive Summary

The AI Protocol That Actually Makes Money

The sovereign mind, rebuilt for an age of machine intelligence.

Most AI projects – many valued in the billions - are betting they grow into a sizable business but this may or may not arrive for most of them. Venice AI already has a business with paying customers, a clever way of returning value to token holders, and one genuine advantage the giants of Silicon Valley cannot copy.

Venice is a generative AI platform - think of it as a privacy-respecting alternative to ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini. You use it the same way: you ask it questions, it writes things for you, it generates images. The difference is what happens to your conversation afterwards. With ChatGPT, your prompts are stored, analysed, and used to train future models. With Venice, nothing is stored at all. The conversation lives only on your own device, and Venice cannot see it, retain it, or hand it over to anyone - because it never had it in the first place.

Erik Voorhees founded Venice in May 2024. He is one of the most respected figures in the Crypto industry, having previously built and run ShapeShift, one of the earliest cryptocurrency exchanges. Two years on from launch, Venice now has over two million registered users, generates 8.8 million website visits a month, and recorded its single best revenue day in the company's history in May 2026.

So far, this could describe any well-run software business. What makes Venice genuinely unusual - and what makes it interesting as an investment rather than just as a product - is the way the company's success is designed to flow back to its token holders.

Every time a customer pays for a Venice subscription, a portion of that dollar revenue is used to buy the company's token (called VVV) on the open market, and these tokens are then permanently destroyed. The more subscriptions Venice sells, the fewer VVV tokens exist. It is the closest thing in Crypto to a traditional share buyback, except it happens automatically, it is visible on the blockchain in real time, and it cannot be quietly paused when management would prefer to keep the cash for itself. To date, nearly 43 per cent of all the VVV tokens that ever existed have been burned this way. The supply keeps shrinking, and the rate of shrinkage accelerates as the platform grows.

Alongside VVV there is a second, more unusual token called DIEM. Each DIEM gives its holder $1 of AI usage on Venice, every day, for as long as the platform exists. Think of it as a season ticket for machine intelligence - a one-time purchase that delivers daily value indefinitely. This matters for two reasons. First, it gives developers and businesses a way to lock in their AI costs at a predictable rate rather than paying a metered bill that rises with usage. Second, every DIEM in existence requires somebody to lock up VVV to create it, which takes more VVV out of circulation and adds to the scarcity pressure.

At a market capitalisation of just $650 million, Venice is valued as a speculative Crypto AI token – that is to say, at an incredible discount to other AI projects outside of Crypto (most of which are years away from real PMF and real revenues). We think this materially undervalues what is actually a working software business with a unique deflationary capital asset dynamic - a structure that genuinely has no clean comparison in either traditional equity markets or in earlier Crypto cycles. The opportunity, in our view, is the gap between how Venice is currently priced and what it would be worth if the wider market understood what it actually is.

1. The Watchtower in the Town Square

Centralised AI is not a private utility. It is a public confession booth where the priest takes notes!

Consider what happens when you ask ChatGPT, Gemini, or Claude a sensitive question - about your health, your finances, a legal worry, an unspoken anxiety, a draft of something you would never publish, a question you might be embarrassed to ask a real person. The exchange feels intimate. It is not. Your prompt, your follow-ups, your edits, your patterns of usage - all of it flows through the servers of one of three or four enormous American corporations, where it is logged, analysed, used to improve future models, and, in many cases, retained indefinitely. Their privacy policies say so. Their terms of service say so. Most users have stopped reading.

This is the great unwritten contract of the modern internet, and we have lived with it for a generation. We accepted it because the alternative seemed impossible. Building a generative AI platform requires staggering quantities of compute, sophisticated model weights, and an engineering bench that only a handful of well-capitalised firms can muster. And so the choice has always been a binary one: surrender your data or do without the tool.

Venice exists because Erik Voorhees decided that binary was a fiction. In a May 2024 essay titled The Separation of Mind and State, Voorhees set out the founding premise: just as Bitcoin separated money from state, machine intelligence must be separated from the corporations and governments that would otherwise own it.

"At the dawn of powerful machine intelligence, we should ask ourselves: what of monopoly control over mind?"

Erik Voorhees, Founder of Venice AI, May 2024

The architectural commitments Venice has made to deliver on that premise are unusually stringent. Prompts and conversations are not stored on Venice servers; chat history lives in the user's local browser and can be deleted at any time, with or without an account. Text and image generation runs through encrypted proxies to a network of decentralised GPU providers - including, since mid-April 2026, Bittensor's Targon subnet (SN4), which Venice now uses for confidential training of its flagship models. Venice cannot see your prompts. Venice cannot retain your prompts. And Venice cannot be compelled to hand over what it does not have.

The Centralised Vulnerability

For most retail users, the privacy argument is a values question - a matter of principle rather than urgency. For institutional, regulated, and sovereign clients, it is something else entirely. A hedge fund cannot run portfolio analysis through a third-party model that retains queries. A law firm cannot draft sensitive correspondence on infrastructure subject to subpoena. A sovereign wealth fund cannot ask a large language model about a confidential mandate without exposing that mandate to a foreign jurisdiction's discovery process. And a multinational pharmaceutical cannot let its drug discovery prompts wash through a competitor's training pipeline.

These are not hypothetical concerns. They are the explicit reasons that enterprise AI procurement has been frozen at most major financial institutions, law firms, and government departments for the past eighteen months. The CIOs we speak with describe a single recurring blocker: the data leaves our perimeter, and we cannot account for where it goes. Venice's architectural promise - that the data never enters anyone else's perimeter in the first place - is not a marketing line. It is the bottleneck that the centralised incumbents cannot unblock without dismantling the business model that makes them profitable.

2. How Venice Actually Works

A simple way to picture it: Venice is to ChatGPT what a Swiss bank is to a high street bank - both hold what you bring them, but one keeps no records and asks no questions while the other logs every transaction for anyone with a court order.

To make sense of Venice as an investment, three things have to be understood: what the platform does, how the two tokens work, and why those tokens accrue value in a genuinely new way rather than being another speculative Crypto wrapper around an ordinary tech product.

The Platform

At its most basic, Venice is a generative AI application - comparable to ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini in user experience. Users can have text conversations, generate images, write code, and increasingly interact with vision and video models. The platform supports the leading open-source models (Llama, Mistral, DeepSeek, and others) and has lately added an in-house flagship called Venice Uncensored 1.2, which was trained end-to-end on Bittensor's Targon subnet rather than centralised cloud infrastructure. Subscriptions sit at three tiers: Pro, Pro+, and Max, paid in fiat currency through Stripe.

So far, this is a familiar story. What makes Venice unusual is the architecture sitting behind the chat window. Inference does not run on Venice's own GPUs; it runs on a network of decentralised compute providers, with prompts routed through encrypted proxies that strip identifying metadata before the model sees the text. There is no central log. There is no usage history bound to an account. There is no advertising model and no data sale, because there is no data to sell.

The Dual-Token Engine

Venice runs on two tokens - VVV and DIEM - that perform very different jobs. The simplest way to keep them straight is to think of VVV as the company's stock and DIEM as a prepaid mobile contract that happens to settle on a blockchain.

VVV is the capital asset. It is the protocol's native token issued on Base blockchain, with an original supply of 100 million, of which approximately 42.9 per cent has already been permanently burned. Holding VVV gives you several things: a claim on the platform's growth through automatic buy-and-burns funded by subscription revenue; a staking yield of roughly 14 to 18 per cent paid in VVV; and the right to lock VVV in order to mint DIEM, which gives access to compute.

DIEM is the access asset. Each DIEM token entitles its holder to $1 of Venice API credit per day, in perpetuity. If you hold one DIEM, you can use $1 of inference today, $1 tomorrow, and $1 every day for as long as Venice exists. DIEM is minted by locking VVV; when DIEM is burned, the locked VVV is released. There is no algorithmic peg, no synthetic stablecoin mechanic, and no dependence on continuous emissions to maintain the relationship. The whole system is bounded by real compute utility.

The Buy-and-Burn

This is the mechanism that, in our view, most distinguishes Venice from every other AI Crypto project on the market. Every Pro subscription triggers a $2 market buy of VVV that is then burned on-chain. Every Pro+ subscription triggers a $5 burn. Every Max subscription triggers a $10 burn. The Sub Burn Programme was upgraded on 27 April 2026 to scale automatically with subscription tier, and every burn is verifiable in real time at venice.ai/token/burns with on-chain transaction links.

The economic effect is direct. Fiat revenue, paid in dollars by ordinary subscribers who may have no Crypto exposure whatsoever, is converted on the open market into a permanent reduction of VVV supply. Venice has further accelerated this by cutting annual emissions by twenty-five per cent in February 2026 - from 8 million to 6 million VVV per year - with a roadmap targeting just 3 million by July 2026. Through additional discretionary burns since November 2025, a total of more than 33.7 million VVV (close to 43 per cent of the original supply) has already been permanently destroyed.

This is a programmatic deflationary mechanism tethered to actual customer revenue. It is what early-stage software equity would look like if Adobe or Salesforce bought back and retired stock every time a new customer signed up - except in Venice's case, it is verifiable, automated, and unable to be quietly suspended in a quarter when management would prefer to flatter earnings.

4. The Five Moats

We believe identifying what makes the business defensible and therefore sustainable should form a central part of a disciplined long-term investment strategy. Venice has at least five moats that make it uncopyable.

I. The Architectural Moat

Venice's privacy guarantees are not a software feature; they are a structural commitment. The centralised incumbents cannot match them without dismantling the data-retention practices that make their advertising and training models profitable. OpenAI cannot credibly promise it will never see your prompts because seeing your prompts is how it improves its product. Google cannot credibly promise it will never log your queries because logged queries are the business. Venice does not face this trade-off because Venice was built without the trade-off in the architecture.

II. The Censorship-Resistance Moat

The mainstream models are subject to a continuous stream of safety patches, refusal layers, jurisdictional content filters, and political guardrails. For most users this is invisible. For a meaningful slice of professional users - security researchers, novelists, academic historians, journalists, medical professionals, regulated traders running scenario analysis - these filters make the tool unusable for legitimate work. Venice's "abliterated" open-source models offer unrestricted text, image, and code generation. The market for this is narrower than the market for general-purpose chat, but it is highly inelastic, well-funded, and almost completely unaddressed by the incumbents.

III. The Revenue-to-Burn Moat

The buy-and-burn engine is not optional, not discretionary, and not reversible. Every subscription burns VVV. As long as Venice signs up customers, supply contracts. This creates a virtuous loop that is structurally different from inflationary Crypto token models: the protocol's growth makes the asset scarcer, not more plentiful. The closest historical analogue is a share buyback funded by operating cash flow - except enforced by smart contract rather than board discretion.

IV. The Composability Moat

Venice now sits at the centre of a decentralised AI stack. Targon (Bittensor SN4) provides the underlying compute for confidential training. Anthropic's Claude Opus 4.7 has been integrated for advanced agentic coding workflows. Venice has also been adopted as a recommended model provider for OpenClaw, the open-source autonomous AI agent platform, since March 2026. Venice has released a set of 19 simple markdown modules that drop straight into any major agent runtime - meaning that every autonomous AI agent built using these skills becomes a continuous, programmatic consumer of Venice inference. This is the "agentic economy" in practice, not in theory.

V. The Founder Moat

Erik Voorhees is the rarest sort of Crypto founder: someone who has built, sold, and operated through multiple full market cycles. He founded ShapeShift in 2014, navigated it through the SEC's evolving stance on registered exchanges, transitioned it to a fully decentralised DAO in 2021, and is now on what amounts to his third major build. Co-founder Teana Baker-Taylor - formerly Vice President at Circle and previously UK Director at Binance - brings a regulatory and policy bench that almost no other AI-Crypto founding team can match. This matters for institutional capital. It matters even more for the kind of operational discipline that will be needed when Venice inevitably faces a regulatory test in one of the larger jurisdictions.

5. The Valuation Gap

Centralised AI commands roughly $12 trillion in enterprise value. Decentralised AI commands roughly $12 billion. The market is currently priced as if the latter will never close the gap. We disagree.

The single most important piece of analysis we have written on the AI sector in the past twelve months is the simple observation that centralised AI controls something like seventy per cent of global cloud infrastructure and trades at an aggregate enterprise value of approximately $12 trillion, while decentralised AI as a whole - every protocol, every token, every subnet, every project - trades at roughly $12 billion. That is a thousand-to-one ratio.

What makes that ratio especially striking is that the price gap is not matched by a performance gap. As we have written about elsewhere, a number of these lowly valued Crypto AI protocols are already matching or even beating the centralised incumbents on state-of-the-art (SOTA) benchmarks - the standardised industry tests that AI labs use to compare how well their models reason, code, understand language, and answer complex questions. A model achieves "SOTA" when it posts the best score yet recorded on one of these tests; in plain English, it is the closest thing the AI industry has to a leaderboard. The implication is significant: the market is currently pricing decentralised AI as if it were technologically inferior. The benchmarks suggest the opposite.

The Targon Integration

In mid-April 2026, Venice shipped Venice Uncensored 1.2, a production-ready, fully uncensored AI model trained end-to-end on Bittensor's Targon subnet (SN4) - not on Amazon Web Services, not on Google Cloud, not on any centralised provider. The upgrade brought vision support, a four-times larger context window, stronger tool-use capabilities, and substantially reduced refusal behaviour. The technical significance is that Venice has now demonstrated that decentralised infrastructure can deliver production-grade model training at the cadence the consumer market expects. The commercial significance is that Venice has reduced its dependence on Big Tech infrastructure to close to zero - exactly the position any sovereign-grade institutional buyer would want their AI provider to occupy.

The Coinbase and Upbit Listings

DIEM went live on the Coinbase application in early 2026, providing a tier-one fiat on-ramp and off-ramp directly into the compute token. VVV gained additional liquidity through listings on Upbit on 12 May 2026 and Crypto.com on 12 May 2026 - meaningful particularly for Korean and Asian retail and institutional access, which has been a persistent gap in the decentralised AI sector.

The Anthropic Opus 4.7 Integration

Venice integrated Anthropic's most advanced model in the Claude family in April 2026, adding support for sophisticated agentic coding workflows. This is a striking signal in two directions: that Venice is being chosen by serious developers building autonomous agents, and that Anthropic is comfortable with Venice's distribution as a privacy-respecting front-end for its models. The competitive logic here is subtle but real - Venice can offer Claude's capability set without Claude's data retention, which addresses the single largest objection enterprise customers have to large-language-model adoption.

The StrikeRobot Partnership

On 11 May 2026, Venice announced a partnership with StrikeRobot to provide AI for humanoid robotics applications. The detail to focus on is not the partnership itself but what it implies about positioning: Venice is being chosen for high-stakes physical applications where privacy is not an abstract principle but a hard requirement. A humanoid robot operating inside a home, watching its occupants and making decisions about them, generates the most sensitive data imaginable. The fact that Venice is being chosen over the centralised alternatives in that context tells you everything about how the privacy moat is being valued by serious operators.

The Wrapped Staked VVV Launch

Venice has also recently introduced Wrapped Staked VVV (wstVVV), a liquid staking derivative built on Lagoon Finance infrastructure that gives holders instant liquidity with auto-compounding yields. The token is transferable and collateral-ready, meaning it can be deployed across the broader decentralised finance ecosystem while continuing to earn the underlying staking yield. This is the kind of infrastructure that signals a protocol maturing from speculative asset into productive collateral - the same trajectory that ETH staking derivatives followed in 2023 and 2024.

7. Risks

We are not in the business of writing one-sided cases. These are the risks we believe matter most.

Regulatory Risk

Venice's privacy and censorship-resistance commitments are commercial strengths and regulatory headwinds in the same breath. The platform's "uncensored" positioning will at some point invite scrutiny - particularly around content moderation in jurisdictions with strict requirements (the United Kingdom's Online Safety Act, the EU's Digital Services Act, several Asian regulatory frameworks). Erik Voorhees has been subject to SEC scrutiny in the past, including a settlement related to ShapeShift's earlier operations. Investors should expect a high probability of at least one significant regulatory test in the next eighteen months.

Tokenomics Complexity Risk

The dual-token VVV-DIEM structure is elegant in design but genuinely complicated to communicate. The risk is not that the mechanics fail; they appear to be working as intended. The risk is that retail investors and even some institutional buyers misprice the relationship between the two tokens, leading to volatile rotation and price action that does not reflect the underlying fundamentals. A simpler structure would be easier to market. Venice has chosen the harder, more durable path, and we believe correctly - but it does carry execution risk in the short term.

Treasury Concentration Risk

Roughly 35 per cent of the original VVV supply was allocated to the Venice treasury, which raises legitimate questions about decentralisation and potential overhang. The team has publicly committed to a cautious approach to monetary policy modifications and has refused to dilute staking requirements simply because the token price has risen - but the structural concentration remains. Investors should monitor treasury wallet activity as a leading indicator of governance discipline.

Competitive Risk

Decentralised AI is a crowded sector. Bittensor, the Artificial Superintelligence Alliance, Morpheus, and a growing list of subnet-level competitors are all targeting overlapping pieces of the market. Venice's distinguishing advantage - its consumer-facing front-end with two million users - is harder to replicate than the underlying infrastructure, but it is not impossible. A well-funded centralised incumbent could, in principle, launch a privacy-first offering that erodes Venice's positioning, although doing so would require dismantling significant parts of their existing business model.

Macro Risk

VVV is a small-cap, highly volatile Crypto asset. It carries all the standard risks of the broader digital asset market - drawdowns of 40 to 60 per cent are not unusual in this sector, and any macro deleveraging event would affect Venice regardless of underlying fundamentals. Position sizing should reflect this. We size Venice as a high-conviction but appropriately capped allocation within a diversified decentralised AI sleeve, not as a concentrated bet.

8. Dragonfly's Approach

Dragonfly Asset Management has been investing across the liquid Crypto landscape for close to four years, and decentralised AI has been our highest-conviction thematic exposure for much of that time. Our process for evaluating protocols like Venice combines fundamental analysis (revenue, user growth, token mechanics, team quality, competitive positioning) with on-chain metrics (holder distribution, staking ratios, burn rates, treasury behaviour) as well as a qualitative assessment of the founding team and its strategy.

We invest exclusively in a diversified basket of liquid tokens. This is a deliberate choice. It gives us the freedom to reallocate as the landscape evolves - towards a new decentralised AI leader, an emerging Bittensor subnet, or an opportunity we have not yet identified. The sector moves fast, and breadth matters; in a category this early, liquidity matters even more.

Within our decentralised AI book, Venice represents what we consider an unusually well-balanced exposure. The combination of demonstrable revenue, programmatic deflation, a defensible architectural moat, a credible founding team, and substantial recent commercial momentum places it in a category we see very rarely. Most assets we evaluate have one or two of these qualities. Venice has all five.

9. Conclusion

Venice is what the early internet was supposed to become before commercial incentives twisted it into something else.

Three decades ago, the founding documents of the internet promised a network that would route around damage, resist centralisation, and put the user in control of their own data. What was built instead was an extractive surveillance architecture dressed in the language of community. We are now several years into the second great wave of computational power - the generative AI wave - and the default architecture is shaping up to look uncomfortably similar to the one we ended up with in the consumer internet.

Venice is one of the few projects we have come across that is meaningfully trying to write a different ending. It is doing so with a working product, real customers, sound token economics, an experienced founding team, and a sectoral tailwind that is unlikely to reverse. The valuation, in our view, reflects the speculative AI narrative rather than the underlying fundamentals - and that gap is precisely the kind of mispricing that makes for an asymmetric investment opportunity.

We continue to hold Venice as an incredibly strong high-conviction token position within our decentralised AI allocation and will be monitoring the metrics - revenue, burns, user growth, agent integrations, regulatory developments - with the rigour the position deserves.
